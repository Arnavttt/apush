Period 1 · 4–6% of Exam
Native America & European Contact
Pre-1491 → 1607

Key Events timeline

  • ~12,000 BCEBering Land Bridge Migration — First Americans cross into the continent; societies develop based on local environment
  • ~1000 CENorse contact — Leif Erikson reaches Vinland; no permanent settlement, no lasting impact
  • 1492Columbus's First Voyage — Arrives in Caribbean; begins permanent European–American contact
  • 1494Treaty of Tordesillas — Spain and Portugal divide the New World; reveals European imperial competition
  • 1519–21Conquest of the Aztec Empire — Hernán Cortés; disease + alliances with enemy tribes explain Spanish victory
  • 1530s–Encomienda System — Forced Native labor for Spanish colonizers; sparks reform debates
  • 1542New Laws of the Indies — Partly responding to Las Casas; attempted to limit Native enslavement

Cause → Effect Chains

Disease kills Natives→Labor shortage→Encomienda system→African slave trade accelerates
Competition between Spain, Portugal, France, England→Race to colonize Americas→Different colonial models

What Your Notes Missed

Spanish vs. French vs. English Models

  • Spanish: Encomienda, mestizo culture, extractive (gold/silver)
  • French: Fur trade, alliances with Natives, less settlement
  • English: Permanent settlement, displace Natives, own land

Syncretism & Cultural Exchange

  • Mestizo (Spanish + Native) culture emerges in New Spain
  • Natives adapted European horses, changing Plains cultures dramatically
  • Europeans adopted Native crops (corn, potatoes) → European population boom

Pueblo Revolt (1680) — OFTEN MISSED

Popé led Pueblo peoples in driving Spanish from New Mexico for 12 years. Shows Natives actively resisted, not just passive victims. Spain returned more cautiously, allowing more cultural autonomy.

Diversity of Native Societies

  • Mississippian city Cahokia (~1100 CE): larger than London at the time
  • Haudenosaunee (Iroquois): democratic confederation with clan mothers
  • Anasazi/Ancestral Puebloans: multi-story stone cities, complex irrigation

⚡ Exam Trap

Bacon's Rebellion is not just a rebellion — it's the pivot point that explains why slavery became entrenched. Elites feared multiracial poor alliances, so they replaced white indentured servants with African enslaved workers who had no legal recourse.

Period 3 · 10–17% of Exam
Revolution & the New Nation
1754–1800

Key Events

  • 1754–63French & Indian War — British win but accumulate massive debt → end of salutary neglect → taxation → Revolution
  • 1763Proclamation of 1763 — Forbids westward settlement past Appalachians; colonists furious
  • 1765Stamp Act — First direct tax on colonists; "No taxation without representation"
  • 1770Boston Massacre — 5 killed; Patriots use as propaganda; Paul Revere's famous engraving deliberately distorted
  • 1773Boston Tea Party — Protest Tea Act monopoly; £10,000 of tea dumped
  • 1774Intolerable Acts — British response; galvanizes colonial unity; First Continental Congress convened
  • 1775Lexington & Concord — "Shot heard 'round the world"; Revolutionary War begins
  • 1776Declaration of Independence — Jefferson; Enlightenment natural rights philosophy; list of grievances against George III
  • 1777Battle of Saratoga — Turning point; France enters as U.S. ally
  • 1781Yorktown — Cornwallis surrenders; war effectively ends
  • 1781–87Articles of Confederation — Weak central government; cannot tax, no executive, no army
  • 1786–87Shays' Rebellion — Massachusetts farmers; shows Articles are failing; triggers Constitutional Convention
  • 1787Constitutional Convention — Philadelphia; replaces Articles; major debates on slavery, representation
  • 1789–97Washington's Presidency — Sets precedents: 2-term limit, cabinet, neutrality, suppress Whiskey Rebellion
  • 1794Whiskey Rebellion — Washington sends troops; proves federal government can enforce laws
  • 1800Election of 1800 — Jefferson defeats Adams; peaceful transfer of power — huge democratic precedent

AP High-Value Insight

The Revolution was politically radical but socially conservative. It changed who ruled (from British king to American elites) but left slavery, gender inequality, and class hierarchy largely intact. AP essays reward this nuance.

What Your Notes Missed

Loyalists — The Other Side

~20% of colonists were Loyalists. Many were merchants, government officials, or recent immigrants. 60,000+ fled to Canada after the Revolution. Their perspective is a great HIPP POV angle.

Role of Enslaved People & Black Americans

Lord Dunmore's Proclamation (1775) offered freedom to enslaved people who fought for Britain — tens of thousands escaped. Some free Black men fought for Patriots. The Revolution deepened the contradiction of "freedom" alongside slavery.

Constitutional Compromises on Slavery

  • 3/5 Compromise — enslaved people count as 3/5 person for representation
  • Slave Trade Clause — Congress cannot ban import until 1808
  • Fugitive Slave Clause — escaped slaves must be returned

Northwest Ordinance (1787)

Organized the territory north of the Ohio River. Banned slavery there (important precedent). Established a process for admitting new states. Often overlooked but appears on the exam.

Democracy, Expansion & Reform
1800–1848

Key Events

  • 1803Louisiana Purchase — Doubles U.S. territory; Jefferson stretches Constitution he claimed to follow strictly
  • 1803Marbury v. Madison — Establishes judicial review; Supreme Court can strike down laws; cornerstone of American government
  • 1812–15War of 1812 — Impressment + trade interference; ends in stalemate; boosts American nationalism; kills Federalist Party
  • 1820Missouri Compromise — Missouri = slave, Maine = free, 36°30' line; first major slavery compromise
  • 1823Monroe Doctrine — Western Hemisphere off-limits to European colonization; defines U.S. sphere of influence

  • 1830Indian Removal Act — Jackson forces Native relocation west of Mississippi; Trail of Tears follows (1838)
  • 1831Nat Turner's Rebellion — Largest slave revolt in U.S. history; 55 whites, 100+ enslaved killed; South tightens slave codes
  • 1830s–40sSecond Great Awakening — Religious revival; fuels abolitionism, women's rights, temperance
  • 1848Seneca Falls Convention — First women's rights convention; Declaration of Sentiments modeled on Declaration of Independence

  • National Road — first federally funded highway

Period 5 · 10–17% of Exam
Civil War & Reconstruction
1844–1877

Key Events

  • 1846–48Mexican-American War — U.S. gains 500,000 sq mi; Wilmot Proviso debates slavery in new territories
  • 1850Compromise of 1850 — California free; Fugitive Slave Act; bought ~4 years of peace
  • 1852Uncle Tom's Cabin (Harriet Beecher Stowe) — Changed Northern public opinion; "the little woman who made this big war" (Lincoln)
  • 1854Kansas-Nebraska Act — Popular sovereignty; repeals Missouri Compromise; creates Republican Party; triggers "Bleeding Kansas"
  • 1857Dred Scott v. Sandford — Scott has no rights; Congress cannot ban slavery anywhere; outraged North; strengthened abolitionism
  • 1859John Brown's Raid on Harpers Ferry — Failed slave revolt; Brown executed; hero in North, terrorist in South; deepens sectional divide
  • 1860Election of Abraham Lincoln — South secedes before he takes office; Democratic Party splits
  • 1861–65Civil War — 620,000+ dead; most destructive American war; preserves Union, ends slavery
  • 1863Emancipation Proclamation — Frees enslaved in Confederate states; war aim shifts; invites Black men to enlist (180,000 serve)
  • 186513th Amendment — Abolishes slavery; also Lincoln assassinated; Johnson becomes president
  • 1867–70Radical Reconstruction — Military districts in South; 14th and 15th Amendments; Black men vote and hold office
  • 1877Compromise of 1877 — Hayes wins disputed election; troops leave South; Reconstruction ends; Jim Crow begins

Key People

Abraham Lincoln
President, 1861–65
Preserved Union; issued Emancipation Proclamation; Gettysburg Address; assassinated by Booth
Harriet Tubman
Underground Railroad
Escaped enslavement; led ~300 others to freedom; Union spy in Civil War
Frederick Douglass
Abolitionist
Argued slavery was incompatible with American ideals; pushed Lincoln toward emancipation
Thaddeus Stevens

Led Radical Republicans in Congress; pushed for Black civil rights and land redistribution ("40 acres and a mule")
Ulysses S. Grant

Andrew Johnson
President, 1865–69
Lenient Reconstruction plan; opposed Radical Republicans; first president impeached (1868)

The 3 Reconstruction Amendments — Must Memorize

13th (1865)

Abolishes slavery and involuntary servitude, except as punishment for crime — that exception is still debated today.

14th (1868)

Citizenship for all born/naturalized in U.S.; equal protection; due process. Later used for women's rights, LGBTQ+ rights, desegregation.

15th (1870)

Cannot deny vote based on race, color, or prior servitude. Does not give women the vote (19th Amendment, 1920).

⚡ Critical Insight — Reconstruction Failure

Reconstruction succeeded legally (amendments, Black officeholders) but failed socially (sharecropping replaced slavery, KKK terrorized communities, Compromise of 1877 ended federal protection). The perfect AP argument: "Legal progress without social enforcement."

Period 6 · 10–17% of Exam
Gilded Age & Industrialization
1865–1898

Key Events

  • 1869Transcontinental Railroad completed — Built by Chinese and Irish immigrants; unifies national market; displaces Native peoples
  • 1877Great Railroad Strike — First nationwide strike; federal troops crush it; shows government favors business
  • 1882Chinese Exclusion Act — First law to ban a nationality; nativism + labor competition; not repealed until 1943
  • 1886Haymarket Affair — Labor rally in Chicago; bomb thrown; 8 anarchists convicted (questionably); labor movement set back
  • 1887Dawes Act — Breaks up tribal lands; forces assimilation; Native land base shrinks by 90 million acres by 1934
  • 1890Sherman Antitrust Act — First federal law regulating monopolies; rarely enforced until Progressives
  • 1890Battle of Wounded Knee — U.S. Army kills 250+ Lakota; effectively ends armed Native resistance
  • 1892Homestead Strike — Carnegie Steel; Pinkerton guards vs. workers; Carnegie's reputation destroyed
  • 1893Panic of 1893 — Major depression; fuels Populist movement
  • 1894Pullman Strike — Eugene Debs; injunction + federal troops; leads to Labor Day as federal holiday
  • 1896Plessy v. Ferguson — "Separate but equal" doctrine; enshrines Jim Crow legally until Brown v. Board (1954)

Key People

Andrew Carnegie
Steel, 1870s–1900
Vertical integration; Gospel of Wealth (rich should give money to society); funded 2,500 libraries
John D. Rockefeller

Samuel Gompers
AFL, 1886
Founded American Federation of Labor; skilled workers only; "pure and simple" unionism (wages, hours)
Jacob Riis
How the Other Half Lives, 1890

William Jennings Bryan

Key Distinction

Robber Baron vs. Captain of Industry — Same person, different perspective. Robber Baron = exploited workers, bribed politicians, crushed competition. Captain of Industry = built economy, created jobs, gave to charity. AP essays reward acknowledging both sides.

Progressive Era through WWII
1890–1945

Key Events

  • 1898Spanish-American War — U.S. becomes imperial power; gains Puerto Rico, Guam, Philippines; debates about imperialism begin
  • 1906The Jungle (Upton Sinclair) — Muckraker novel about meatpacking; led to Meat Inspection Act and Pure Food & Drug Act
  • 191316th and 17th Amendments — Income tax (16th); Direct election of senators (17th) — both Progressive Era reforms
  • 1914Federal Reserve Act (1913) — Wilson; central bank controls money supply; stabilizes economy
  • 1917U.S. enters WWI — Zimmermann Telegram + unrestricted submarine warfare; "make the world safe for democracy"
  • 1918–19Great Migration begins — 1.6 million Black Americans move North for jobs and escape Jim Crow; reshapes American cities
  • 191918th Amendment (Prohibition) — Bans alcohol; 19th Amendment (1920) gives women the vote
  • 1920sHarlem Renaissance — Black cultural flowering in NYC; Langston Hughes, Zora Neale Hurston, Louis Armstrong; Black identity
  • 1929Stock Market Crash (Black Tuesday) — Begins Great Depression; 25% unemployment; Hoover does too little
  • 1933–38New Deal (FDR) — Relief, Recovery, Reform; Social Security (1935); FDIC; Wagner Act (labor rights); expands federal government permanently
  • 1941Pearl Harbor — Dec. 7; U.S. enters WWII; Japanese internment (Executive Order 9066) follows
  • 1944D-Day / GI Bill — Normandy invasion; GI Bill funds veterans' education and housing → postwar middle class
  • 1945Atomic bombs on Japan — Hiroshima and Nagasaki; WWII ends; nuclear age begins; U.S. as global superpower

Progressive Era — What Your Notes Missed

Muckrakers — Know These

  • Ida Tarbell — Exposed Standard Oil monopoly
  • Upton Sinclair — The Jungle (meatpacking)
  • Jacob Riis — Urban tenement photography
  • Lincoln Steffens — Political machine corruption

Limitations of Progressivism

  • Most Progressives were white, middle-class, and excluded Black Americans
  • Eugenics movement was mainstream among Progressives
  • Immigration restrictions (1921, 1924) — quota laws targeting Southern/Eastern Europeans
  • Wilson resegregated federal government

WWII Home Front — Often Tested

Japanese Internment

110,000 Japanese Americans in camps (Executive Order 9066); Korematsu v. U.S. (1944) upheld it; formally apologized in 1988

Nonviolent Direct Action (MLK / SCLC)

  • Sit-ins (Greensboro, 1960)
  • Freedom Rides (1961)

Recurring Continuities to Mention in Any Essay

Racial Inequality

Persists in different forms from slavery → Black Codes → Jim Crow → mass incarceration. Legal progress has never matched social reality.

Tension Over Federal Power

States' rights vs. federal authority runs from Anti-Federalists → nullification → secession → states' rights (civil rights era) → today.

Westward / Global Expansion

Continental expansion (Manifest Destiny) → overseas imperialism → Cold War containment → War on Terror. Always justified by "civilization," "democracy," or "security."

Historical Reasoning Skill
Causation Patterns
AP Essay Tip

AP readers want multiple causes and effects, not just one. Use political, economic, and social/ideological causes. Then distinguish immediate causes from underlying ones — that earns complexity points.

American Revolution

  • Political: No taxation without representation; colonial assemblies
  • Economic: Mercantilist restrictions hurt merchants
  • Ideological: Enlightenment (Locke, natural rights, social contract)
  • Trigger: Intolerable Acts and Lexington/Concord

Civil War

  • Immediate: Lincoln's election, Southern secession
  • Long-term: Slavery expansion debate (1820–1860)
  • Economic: Industrial North vs. slave-based South
  • Political: Failure of compromise (Kansas-Nebraska, Dred Scott)

Great Depression

  • Stock market speculation + buying on margin
  • Overproduction in agriculture and industry
  • Bank failures (no FDIC yet)
  • Smoot-Hawley Tariff → global trade collapse
  • Drought (Dust Bowl) worsens farm crisis

Civil Rights Movement

  • WWII veterans returned demanding rights (Double V)
  • Cold War embarrassment — USSR pointed to U.S. racism
  • Great Migration → Northern Black political power
  • Television broadcast violence against protesters
  • Legal groundwork: NAACP lawsuits from 1930s

DBQ · LEQ · SAQ

DBQ Rubric — 7 Points Total

Document-Based Question (DBQ)

1Thesis/Claim — Historically defensible thesis that responds to the prompt (not just a restatement). Must include a line of reasoning beyond "agree/disagree."
1Contextualization — Describe a broader historical context BEFORE or AFTER the period. Must be a full paragraph, not a single sentence. Connect it to the argument.
2Evidence — Content: use 3+ docs accurately (1 pt). Beyond Description: explain how 6+ docs support your argument (1 pt). Not just summary — explain HOW each doc supports the thesis.
3Analysis & Reasoning — HIPP sourcing on 3+ docs (1 pt) + Demonstrate complexity (1 pt) + Corroboration/CCOT/Comparison across docs (1 pt).

LEQ Rubric — 6 Points Total

Long Essay Question (LEQ)

1Thesis — Clear, defensible claim that establishes a line of reasoning. Must be more than a restatement.
1Contextualization — Describe broader context and connect to argument. Full paragraph minimum.
2Evidence — Specific examples used (1 pt) + Evidence used to support argument (1 pt). Name specific events, people, laws — not vague references.
2Analysis & Reasoning — Use a historical reasoning skill (causation, CCOT, comparison) throughout the essay (1 pt) + Demonstrate complexity (1 pt).

HIPP Analysis — Document Analysis Framework

HIPP Framework — Use on Every Document
H
Historical Context
What was happening in the world WHEN this was written? Events preceding or surrounding the document. Not just "during the ___ period."
I
Intended Audience
Who was this written FOR? Knowing the audience explains WHY certain things are emphasized or omitted. Different audiences = different rhetoric.
P
Purpose
WHY was this created? To persuade, justify, celebrate, criticize? Purpose shapes what the author includes and excludes. Link to specific words or claims.
P
Point of View
How does the author's identity (occupation, class, race, gender, nationality) shape their perspective? What do they believe that others might not?
Model sentence: "Because [author] was [identity/role] who [context], their [document type] emphasizes [specific claim] in order to [purpose], which [may not reflect / does reflect] the experience of [other group]."

Complexity Point — How to Earn It

Four Ways to Show Complexity

  • Corroborate: Multiple causes/effects reinforce your argument
  • Qualify: Show how another group's experience complicates your thesis
  • Modify: Show how the argument changes across time/region
  • Explain tension: Identify contradictions within the period

Complexity Sentence Starters

  • "While [Group A] experienced [X], [Group B] experienced [Y], demonstrating that…"
  • "Although [Argument], this did not apply uniformly because…"
  • "This change was more pronounced in [region/group] than in [other]…"
  • "This argument is complicated by the fact that [counterexample]…"

Thesis Writing — The Formula

Thesis Formula

A strong thesis = [Time period] + [Specific cause/change] + [Effect/argument] + [Why it matters / line of reasoning].

Weak: "The American Revolution changed American society."
Strong: "While the American Revolution created a republican government based on Enlightenment principles, it reinforced social hierarchies by leaving slavery, gender inequality, and class structures largely unchanged, revealing that the Revolution was more political than social in its impact."

The 9 APUSH Themes

NAT — American Identity

Who is "American"? Debates over inclusion/exclusion of Natives, immigrants, women, Black Americans. Changes with each era.

POL — Politics & Power

Who governs? Federal vs. state power, expansion of democracy, political parties, reform. Recurring tension throughout.

WXT — Work, Exchange, Technology

How does the economy function? From mercantilism → industrial capitalism → corporate capitalism → globalization.

CUL — Culture & Society

How do values, arts, religion shape society? Great Awakenings, Harlem Renaissance, counterculture, culture wars.

MIG — Migration & Settlement

Movement of people: Native migrations, European colonization, immigration waves, Great Migration, suburbanization.

GEO — Geography & Environment

How does land shape society? Westward expansion, Manifest Destiny, conservation movement, environmental movement.

WOR — America in the World

Isolationism → imperialism → global superpower → War on Terror. Foreign policy motivations and consequences.

ARC — American Regional Culture

North vs. South vs. West differences in economy, society, politics. Sectionalism persists through today.

SOC — Social Structures

Class, race, gender hierarchies. Who has power? How do they maintain or challenge it? Reform vs. backlash cycle.

Frequently Confused Concepts

Popular Sovereignty vs. States' Rights

Popular sovereignty = voters in a territory decide an issue (e.g., slavery — Kansas-Nebraska Act). States' rights = state governments have power over federal government. Both were used to defend slavery, but they are different concepts.

Isolationism vs. Neutrality vs. Non-Interventionism

Isolationism = avoid all foreign entanglements (Washington's Farewell Address, 1930s). Neutrality = don't take sides in a war. Non-interventionism = don't intervene militarily, but trade with all. These are often used interchangeably on the exam.

Vertical vs. Horizontal Integration

Vertical integration (Carnegie) = own every step of production (mine → ship → factory → retailer). Horizontal integration (Rockefeller) = buy out all competitors in the same industry. Both create monopolies; one controls process, one controls market.

New Deal "Three Rs"

Relief = immediate help for unemployed (CCC, WPA). Recovery = restart the economy (AAA, TVA). Reform = prevent future crashes (FDIC, Social Security, Wagner Act). An AP essay should categorize programs by which R they serve.

SAQ Practice Answers — High-Scoring Models

How to Write a Perfect SAQ (3 sentences minimum per part)

Sentence 1: State your answer directly. Sentence 2: Provide specific evidence (name, event, date, document). Sentence 3: Explain the significance — connect to a broader theme or cause/effect.

Example prompt: "Briefly describe ONE way the First Great Awakening influenced colonial society."
Answer: The First Great Awakening challenged the authority of established religious institutions by promoting emotional, individual religious experiences. For example, itinerant preachers like George Whitefield drew crowds across colonial lines, creating a shared experience that transcended regional differences. This challenge to traditional authority helped cultivate an individualistic mindset that would later fuel resistance to British political authority.
